## What You Are Building

A new habit, not a file. By the end of this lab you will know how to turn on plan mode, read what Claude is proposing, push back when something is off, and approve when it is right. This is the single most important habit for anyone using Claude Code on work that actually matters.

Plan mode is Claude saying "let me think first, then I will act, but only with your approval." Nothing changes until you say go.

> **At work, this comes up when you're...**
>
> - about to run something that affects real documents or systems
> - working on something high-stakes where a mistake costs you time or trust
> - handing Claude a complex task with multiple steps and wanting to check the approach
> - new to Claude Code and still building instinct for what to trust

## Why This Matters

Most people learn Claude Code by jumping in and asking for things. That works for small, safe tasks. It does not work when you are touching something important.

Plan mode slows Claude down to your speed. It reads the situation, proposes a plan in plain English, and waits. You read the plan the same way you would read a colleague's proposal before saying yes. You catch the thing that was off. You ask for the change. You approve when it is right.

This is agent management at its purest. You are not writing prompts. You are reviewing a proposal.

## Pick a task worth thinking about

> **Hint:** _Plan mode is most useful when the task has more than one step, touches something important, or you do not fully trust your own first instinct about how to approach it._

Think of something you actually want Claude's help with right now. Good candidates:

- Drafting a message you need to send to a stakeholder
- Reorganizing notes or a document that matters
- Researching a topic where you are not sure where to start
- Preparing materials for a meeting
- Making a recommendation you will need to defend

The task should feel real. Not a made-up test. Plan mode is a habit, and habits stick when you use them on actual work.

> **You're there when:** you have a one-sentence description of the task you want help with.

---

## Turn on plan mode

> **Hint:** _Plan mode is a keyboard shortcut inside Claude Code. You can turn it on before you start, or switch into it mid-conversation._

In your Claude Code window, press **Shift + Tab** twice. The first press switches you to auto-accept mode. The second press switches you to plan mode. You will see an indicator at the bottom of the window that reads **plan mode on**.

That is it. You are now in plan mode.

**What changed:** Claude will read, think, and propose, but it will not make any changes until you approve. Nothing happens in the background. You are in the driver's seat.

> **You're there when:** you see "plan mode on" at the bottom of your Claude Code window.

---

## Describe the task and wait for the plan

> **Hint:** _Describe the task the way you would describe it to a new colleague. Claude will ask clarifying questions if something is unclear. Answer those questions fully._

Tell Claude what you want to do. Be natural, not technical. Example:

```
I want to draft a one-page recommendation for my team about whether to extend our partnership with Vendor X for another year. I have notes from the last review. The audience is my director and the two program leads. Help me put this together.
```

Claude may ask clarifying questions. Answer them. When Claude has enough to work with, it will propose a plan. The plan might look like this:

```
Here is my plan:
1. Read your notes from the last vendor review
2. Identify the strongest arguments for and against renewal
3. Draft the recommendation in three sections: context, assessment, recommendation
4. Match the tone to something you would share with a director (direct, fact-based, short)
5. Show you the draft and wait for your feedback before making any changes

Ready to proceed?
```

Do not approve yet. Read it first.

> **You're there when:** Claude has shown you a plan and is waiting for your approval.

---

## Read the plan like a colleague's proposal

> **Hint:** _You are reviewing, not reading. Ask the questions you would ask if a teammate handed you a one-pager: is this the right approach, is anything missing, is anything wrong?_

Go through the plan and ask yourself:

- **Does the approach match what I actually want?** If the plan treats this as a research task but I really need a decision memo, say so.
- **Is anything missing?** If the plan skips a step you care about (like checking a specific source, or using a specific format), say so.
- **Is anything wrong?** If the plan makes an assumption that is not right (like "my audience is senior leadership" when it is actually peers), say so.
- **Is the scope right?** If the plan is bigger than you needed or smaller than you needed, say so.

Most plans will not be perfect on the first pass. That is the point. You are here to catch the mismatch before any real work happens.

> **You're there when:** you have read the plan closely and noticed at least one thing you would tweak.

---

## Push back and refine

> **Hint:** _Plain English is enough. You do not need a special format or syntax to give feedback. Just say what you want different._

Tell Claude what you want to change. Some examples:

```
The plan is good but missing a step. I also want you to compare this renewal against what we got from Vendor Y last year, which I will share with you.
```

```
The tone should be less formal. Same facts, but written the way I actually talk.
```

```
Skip the assessment section. I just need the context and the recommendation. Two sections, half a page total.
```

Claude will update the plan. You may go back and forth two or three times before it feels right. That is normal and expected. Each round of push-back is you teaching Claude what you actually want, which is faster than writing the thing yourself.

When the plan is what you want, approve it. Claude will execute the plan and stay in plan mode going forward. If you want to exit plan mode later, press **Shift + Tab** again.

> **You're there when:** you have pushed back at least once and Claude has revised the plan.

---

## Use plan mode three more times this week

> **Hint:** _Plan mode is a muscle. You build it by using it on small and medium tasks until it feels automatic. The habit is what matters, not any single use._

Before the week is out, use plan mode on three different tasks. Try it on:

1. Something small and safe, so you get used to the flow
2. Something medium, where getting it wrong would be annoying but recoverable
3. Something high-stakes, where plan mode is doing real risk-reduction for you

After each one, ask yourself:

- Did the plan catch something I would have missed if I had jumped in?
- Did pushing back on the plan get me to a better outcome?
- Would I trust Claude to do this without plan mode next time, or would I still want to review?

If you would not trust it without plan mode yet, keep using plan mode. Trust builds through reps, not theory.

> **You're done when:** you have used plan mode three times and you can articulate when you would use it and when you would not.

## What You Just Did

You just built the habit of reviewing a proposal before any work happens. That is agent management. Every time you use plan mode from now on you are doing four things at once:

1. **Catching mistakes** before they happen, not after.
2. **Teaching Claude** what your standards are through the feedback you give.
3. **Building trust** in your own judgment about when Claude needs supervision and when it does not.
4. **Saving time** even though it feels slower, because revising a plan is faster than cleaning up the wrong output.

This habit will make every other lab in this catalog safer and more effective.

## What Not to Do

1. **Do not skip plan mode on anything that actually matters.** The cost of reviewing a plan is seconds. The cost of fixing a bad output is minutes or hours.
2. **Do not approve a plan just to be polite.** If something is off, say so. That is the whole point of the mode.
3. **Do not expect the first plan to be right.** The first pass is a starting point, not an answer.
4. **Do not leave plan mode on for everything forever.** For small, trusted tasks you have done a hundred times, plan mode is overkill. Learn when to turn it off.
5. **Do not treat pushback as failure.** Pushback is the learning loop. Every round teaches Claude what good looks like for you.

## Next Steps

Once plan mode feels natural, move on to:

- **Planning with Claude** (easy) — Take the habit of plan mode and apply it to full project planning, not just single tasks.
- **Claude as a Thought Partner** (easy) — Flip the relationship. Instead of Claude doing things for you, have Claude help you think through things.
- **Build Your First Skill** (easy) — Turn a planning pattern you use often into a reusable tool.
